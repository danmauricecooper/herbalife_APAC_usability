Methodology

A three-phase mixed-methods approach allowed us to measure behavioral differences quantitatively while understanding the "why" behind those differences through qualitative observation.

Phase 1
A/B Testing - Product Display Pages
Participants compared two versions of product pages with different information hierarchies and content emphasis. We measured preference, comprehension, and purchase confidence.
Sample Size: 94 participants
Duration: 8-10 minutes per session
Metrics: Preference rate, time to decision, confidence scores
Phase 2
Tree Sorting - Information Architecture
Users organized 35 Herbalife products into their own intuitive category structures. This revealed how APAC buyers naturally group products versus US-designed taxonomies.
Sample Size: 94 participants
Duration: 12-15 minutes per session
Analysis: Similarity matrices, dendrogram clustering
Phase 3
Unmoderated Usability Testing
Participants completed realistic purchase scenarios while thinking aloud, revealing friction points, decision-making patterns, and expectations throughout the buying journey.
Sample Size: 94 participants
Duration: 20-25 minutes per session
Scenarios: 3 task-based scenarios per participant

Key Insights

Our research revealed five critical differences between APAC and US buying behaviors, each with clear implications for product design and localization strategy.

1
Social Proof Trumps Expert Authority

APAC buyers prioritized customer reviews and testimonials significantly more than US buyers, who showed stronger preference for expert endorsements and clinical studies. In A/B testing, product pages emphasizing peer reviews outperformed expert-led pages by 34% in APAC markets, while the pattern reversed in US comparisons.

This extended to visual hierarchy: APAC participants consistently scrolled to reviews first, while US participants focused on ingredient lists and certifications. Tree sorting revealed APAC users grouped products by "most popular" and "highest rated," while US users organized by "product type" and "health goal."

2
Payment Method Expectations Create Conversion Barriers

One of the most critical friction points emerged during checkout: APAC users expected region-specific payment methods that weren't available in the current implementation. 68% of participants expressed confusion or concern when they couldn't find familiar payment options.

Indonesia: 82% of participants expected to see GoPay, OVO, or Dana as payment options. Several abandoned mock checkouts when only credit cards were offered, with one participant saying, "I don't use credit cards for online shopping‚Äîthis feels unsafe."

Malaysia: 71% looked for Touch 'n Go eWallet or Grab Pay. Participants described PayPal as "outdated" and preferred mobile wallet integration.

Philippines: 64% expected GCash or PayMaya options. Credit card hesitancy was particularly high, with participants citing security concerns and preference for load-based payments.

Taiwan: 59% expected LINE Pay or JKO Pay integration, though credit card acceptance was higher than other markets.

3
Checkout Flow Expectations Diverge Significantly

The linear, streamlined checkout flow optimized for US users created frustration for APAC participants who expected different patterns and more flexibility throughout the purchase process.

Order Review Anxiety: 76% of APAC participants repeatedly returned to previous checkout steps to verify their selections, compared to 31% in US baseline data. The one-page checkout that tested well in the US felt "too fast" and "not secure enough" for APAC users who wanted clear stage-by-stage confirmation.

Guest Checkout Resistance: 83% of participants preferred to create accounts before purchasing, contrary to US patterns favoring guest checkout. Users cited concerns about tracking orders, accessing customer service, and building relationships with the brand. The prominent "Continue as Guest" option that reduces US friction actually increased APAC abandonment.

Price Transparency Issues: 64% of participants expressed confusion about final pricing, expecting to see detailed breakdowns including taxes, fees, and shipping costs much earlier in the flow. Several participants abandoned when these weren't visible on the cart page.

Contact Information Timing: Participants wanted to provide phone numbers and preferred contact methods at the beginning of checkout, not at the end. 71% expressed that "they should ask for my phone number first in case there's a problem with delivery."

4
Language Localization Beyond Translation

While the site was translated into local languages, numerous language-related usability issues emerged that went deeper than word-for-word translation‚Äîrevealing gaps in cultural adaptation and linguistic nuance.

Measurement Systems: Product descriptions using imperial measurements (ounces, pounds) created confusion across all markets. 89% of participants couldn't easily convert or estimate product sizes, leading to uncertainty about value and serving sizes. One Indonesian participant said, "I have no idea if 16 ounces is a lot or a little."

Tone and Formality: Marketing copy translated directly from English felt either too casual or inappropriately formal depending on the market. Malaysian and Indonesian participants noted that some product descriptions felt "too American" and didn't match local communication styles for health products.

Terminology Inconsistency: Product categories and filters used different terms across pages, creating confusion during navigation. Junior researchers analyzing session recordings identified 23 instances where participants searched for products using different vocabulary than the site's taxonomy.

Ingredient Names: Scientific ingredient names were often untranslated, leaving participants uncertain about what they were buying. 67% of users cross-referenced ingredient lists with external search engines, adding friction to the purchase decision.

Call-to-Action Clarity: Button labels like "Add to Cart" and "Checkout" tested poorly in literal translations. Participants expected more specific, action-oriented language that communicated next steps and commitment levels more clearly.

Bundle and Subscription Mental Models Differ

APAC participants organized products around usage occasions and bundled solutions, while US taxonomy emphasized individual products and subscription models. In tree sorting, 78% of APAC users created categories like "morning routine set" and "weight loss program," demonstrating a preference for holistic, goal-oriented product groupings.

Subscription offers tested poorly across all APAC markets (18% interest vs. 54% in US), with participants expressing preference for flexible, one-time purchases and the ability to mix-and-match products. The "Subscribe & Save" model that drives US conversion actually increased purchase anxiety in APAC contexts.

Research Opportunities & Recommendations

These insights pointed to clear opportunities for localization and further research to optimize conversion across APAC markets.

Payment Gateway Integration
Integrate region-specific mobile wallet payment options (GoPay, OVO, Touch 'n Go, GCash, LINE Pay) into checkout flow. Test impact on cart abandonment rates and conversion across each market.
High Impact Q1 Priority
Multi-Step Checkout Redesign
Prototype and test a staged checkout flow with clear progress indicators, early price transparency, and account-first (vs. guest-first) orientation. Validate whether stepped approach reduces anxiety and increases completion.
High Impact Q1-Q2 Priority
Cultural Copy Adaptation
Partner with in-market copywriters to rewrite product descriptions, CTAs, and marketing content using culturally appropriate tone, terminology, and measurement systems. Test comprehension and purchase confidence improvements.
High Impact Ongoing
Review Infrastructure Enhancement
Redesign PDPs to prominently feature customer reviews, ratings, and testimonials above-the-fold. Conduct follow-up testing on optimal review display formats and social proof mechanisms for each market.
Medium Impact Q2 Priority
Bundle & Program Navigation
Restructure product taxonomy to emphasize goal-based bundles and wellness programs rather than individual products. Test whether this matches APAC mental models and improves findability.
Medium Impact Q2-Q3 Priority
Ingredient Localization Study
Research how to present ingredient information in locally meaningful ways‚Äîtranslating scientific names, providing cultural context for unfamiliar ingredients, and addressing regional health concerns proactively.

Impact & Outcomes
